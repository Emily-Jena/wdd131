The different Type of Beads

Glass Beads

The world of glass beads offers a dazzling array, from miniscule seed beads just millimeters in size to impressive hand-blown lampwork beads reaching an inch or two. Transparency and opacity provide versatility, while some beads boast captivating internal decorations like crackles or color streaks. Others feature vibrant exterior coatings, often with an iridescent sheen for added luster. Beyond smooth and faceted options, glass beads come in a multitude of pressed shapes formed in molds. Crystal beads, particularly sought-after, contain a touch of lead that fuels their exceptional sparkle and brilliance. As with any art form, prices for glass beads fluctuate significantly, influenced by size, quality, and production methods.

Plastic Beads

Plastic beads are a crafter's dream come true - readily available in craft stores and even supermarkets. They reign supreme in affordability, often sold in bulk packs of hundreds, making them perfect for cost-conscious projects. Lightweight and vibrant, plastic beads are a safe and delightful choice for children's creations. Manufactured in molds, they may have a noticeable seam running around the center and typically boast large threading holes for easy stringing. Offered in both transparent and opaque finishes, they might not possess the sparkle of glass, but their cheerful colors make them ideal for playful, layered elastic bracelets.

Wooden Beads

Size:

Similar to plastic beads, wooden options offer a delightful combination of beauty and lightweight ease of use. They typically come in classic shapes like rounds, ovals, tubes, and rondelles. Wood's versatility shines through its natural state or vibrant dyes, allowing for diverse creations with the same material. The larger threading holes make them perfect for threading with leather cord and ideal for young crafters. Unlike many other bead options, wooden beads tend to be chunkier in size, with a wider range of larger options readily available. Whether you find them in bulk packs or pre-strung on 16-inch strands, wooden beads are an affordable and accessible way to begin your beading adventure.

Metal Beads

Metal beads offer a touch of sophistication to your creations. Typically crafted from thin sheets of metal stamped into molds, they come in various styles. You can find plain, high-polish options for a sleek look, or explore patterned varieties with ribs, flutes, or even raised designs. Enamel beads add another dimension, featuring a metal core coated in vibrant enamel. The world of metal beads caters to different budgets, with options in base metals, precious metals, and plated metals. From classic silver and gold finishes to antiqued tones, you're sure to find the perfect match for your project. Sizes typically range from a tiny 2mm to a around 16mm, and unsurprisingly, the price reflects the material and size of the bead.

Ceramic and Polymer Clay Beads

Ceramic beads, often glazed and hand-painted with intricate designs, add a touch of tradition (especially those from China and India). Polymer clay beads, on the other hand, boast vibrant colors throughout the material itself and come in various shapes, both glazed and unglazed. Both offer a range of designs, from abstract to figurative. While ceramic beads are typically sold in smaller packs, both are perfect for beginners, offering a foundation for creative exploration before diving into the vast world of specialty bead types.

Stone Beads

Size:

Stone beads come in a captivating array of shapes, from tiny chips and nuggets to smooth classics and intricately faceted rondelles. Denser than other materials, they offer a comforting weight and warmth against the skin. However, their threading holes can be small, so checking size before purchase is crucial. Standard round and oval stones typically range from 4mm to 8mm. While real stone prices, especially for semi-precious varieties, can be high, irregular or large (think 3-4cm) designs are often more affordable, especially in readily available stones like rose quartz or onyx. Sold in 16-inch strands (with some variation), you'll get more beads for your buck with smaller sizes. Remember, price reflects both the stone type and its cut - expect to pay more for faceted beauties compared to chips and nuggets.
